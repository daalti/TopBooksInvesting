Greatest Books for Investing in 2022

Investing is a successful approach to use your money and possibly increase your fortune . Your money may grow in value and outpace inflation if you make wise investment decisions. The Little Book that Beats the Market

The Little Book that Beats the Market is the first book on the list and, in my opinion, the first book a novice trader should read. Joel Greenblatt, the manager of the $5.6 billion hedge fund Gotham Funds, is the author of the book, which was initially published in 2006. Don't be misled by the book's 112 pages—it truly earns its title. The Magic Formula, which is perhaps my favorite stock screening method, is revealed in The Little Book That Beats the Market.

The price/earnings ratio and returns on assets (or capital) are two crucial performance indicators that the Magic Formula uses to identify promising stocks (or earnings yield). Joel Greenblatt provides an insightful and humorous explanation of why these two KPIs are necessary for effective stock market investment. While reading the book, I frequently found myself laughing aloud since it is so outrageous and direct at moments. And let me tell you, that's really unusual for a book about investing. Take, for example, this passage from the book. Individual stock selection is equivalent to scurrying through a dynamite plant with a blazing match without any understanding of what you're looking for. You may still be alive, but you're a moron. This book is incredibly fun to read because it's around 60% educational and 40% humorous. I should also mention that the Magic Formula screener is available at magicformulainvesting.com for free usage as an additional resource.

One up on Wall Street

Buy on Amazon
Buy on Amazon

One up on Wall Street, written by Peter Lynch, is the second book on the list and is similarly a more relaxed read. It was first released in 1989.

Legendary Fidelity Investments investor Peter Lynch oversaw the management of the Magellan Fund from 1977 to 1990. He increased the fund's value from $18 million to $14 billion over this time, averaging a 29.2% yearly return. Given how absurd that is, it is hardly surprising that more than a million copies of the book have been sold globally. The book is undoubtedly longer than The Little Book that Beats the Market at 334 pages, but it's still a fairly simple read.

The book advocates using what you already know to profit in the stock market, and for me, the following passage from the book served as somewhat of a wake-up call:

In general, if you polled all the doctors, I’d bet only a small percentage would turn out to be invested in medical stocks, and more would be invested in oil; and if you polled the shoe-store owners, more would be invested in aerospace than in shoes, while the aerospace engineers are more likely to dabble in shoe stocks. Why it is that stock certificates, like grasses, are always greener in somebody else’s pasture, I’m not sure.

The book's chapters 8 and 9 are its high points. In chapter 8, Peter Lynch talks about the 13 characteristics of a "tenbagger", which he uses to describe equities with a potential gain of tenfold. He highlights six characteristics of the inverted tenbagger in chapter 9, alluding to equities that are destined for the trash. Some of the qualities of a tenbagger he describes in chapter 8 are amusing and rather illogical, like:

1. The institutions don’t own it, and the analysts don’t follow it

2. It’s in a no-growth industry

3. And best of all … There’s something depressing about it

The University of Berkshire Hathaway is listed as number 3 on this list. Warren Buffett's company, Berkshire Hathaway, is one of the biggest publicly traded businesses in existence. Because the author Daniel Pecaut has spent almost 30 years listening to Warren Buffett and his right-hand man Charlie Munger talk about investing at Berkshire Hathaway's annual shareholder meetings, the book's title is highly relevant. The book provides a summary of these 30 years. The book has 338 pages, making it nearly identical in length as One Up on Wall Street but being slightly more complex. It was only released in 2017, so it's also really recent.

Truly, Charlie Munger and Warren Buffett are two brilliant individuals. Additionally, they are humorous in addition to being brilliant. Just think about what they say when they hear wealthy individuals in western nations gripe about high taxes:

We think — at least I think I’m extraordinarily well treated by this society, and I think most people with high incomes are. I think if you transported most of them to Bangladesh or Peru or something, they would find out how much of it is them and how much is the society. Or sometimes they are just really blunt, which is great too.

Such as when Charlie Munger is asked what he thinks about the GAP figure EBITDA which appears in stock market companies’ income statements.

Yeah, I think you would understand any presentation using the word EBITDA, if every time you saw that word you just substituted the phrase, “bullsh*t earnings.

The University of Berkshire Hathaway contains highlights of the annual shareholder meetings from 1986 to 2017 annual shareholder meetings from 1986 to 2017 in the form of notes. Visit the Warren Buffett Archive on CNBC to view the meetings from 1994 to 2020 for yourself. This is a fantastic resource, however because the meetings go roughly five hours each, there will be a total of 135 hours of watch time. This book condenses the same material in just ten pages.

Common Stocks and Uncommon Profits

Buy on Amazon

Common Stocks and Uncommon Profits by Philip Fisher is the fourth book I'd advise anyone to read. Warren Buffett is frequently credited as being 85% Benjamin Graham and 15% Philip Fisher, and Buffett was particularly influenced by this 1958 book. The book is only 200 pages long, yet it contains some harder lessons. They aren't more technically or mathematically challenging than anything you'll find in the previous three volumes, but it can be challenging to put them into practice. Charlie Munger will give you the main points of the book as follows:

The basic idea of that it was hard to find good stocks, and it was hard to find good investments, and that you wanted to be in good investments. And therefore, you just find a few of them that you knew a lot about, and concentrate on those, it seemed to me such an obviously good idea. And indeed, it’s proved to be an obviously good idea. Yet, 98 percent of the investing world doesn’t follow it. That’s been good for us. It’s been good for you.

Warren Buffett was first introduced to the concept of investing in great firms and hanging onto them indefinitely by Phil Fisher. Fisher also put his teachings into practice. He supposedly hung on to his Motorola investment for decades, along with those of his clients, until it turned into a 2000-bagger. With a 2000-bagger, you can become a billionaire with just $500 invested. One of the book's high points is when Phil Fisher explains how regular investors might use resources from the real world to outperform Wall Street. He counsels serious investors to research firms by speaking to their competitors, suppliers, consumers, employees, and ex-workers. According to him, if you spoke with the top five businesses in a certain industry and had them individually describe the other four, you would be able to tell who the fiercest competitor is.

The Intelligent Investor by Benjamin Graham is item number 5 on this list. This list would not have been possible without it, as we all know. I'm not quite impartial, after all. The founder of value investing, Benjamin Graham, also trained Warren Buffett. Although I would contend that it is not the most comprehensive work, The Intelligent Investor is his most well-known publication.

The book is more technical than the books that came before it on this list, but given that it was first published in 1949, it has undoubtedly withstood the test of time. However, if you don't have at least some investment expertise, you generally won't get the most out of it. Additionally, it has 640 pages.Three main points are made throughout the book:

- Intrinsic value: Because a stock is an ownership interest in a company, it has an intrinsic (or actual) value.

- Mr. Market: The stock market fluctuates between being overly gloomy and enthusiastic. True investors are unaffected by Mr. Market's volatile emotions and just view price increases and decreases as opportunities rather than as a source of knowledge.

- Margin of Safety: When making decisions in the stock market, a margin of safety must always be considered. Thus, you must insist on purchasing stocks at a significant discount to their fundamental (or true) business worth.

The Essays of Warren Buffett

The sixth book on the list, "The Essays of Warren Buffett", was written by Lawrence Cunningham and compiles the key points from all of Berkshire Hathaway's annual shareholder letters. These letters were written by Buffett himself, and the book was released in 1997. Despite the book's 328 pages, there is a ton of content. Buffett discusses a wide range of topics, including what it takes to become a great investor, mergers and acquisitions, what a seedy place Wall Street is, how newspapers have evolved, human psychology, the failure of academics in finance, the value of contrarianism, and back to what a seedy place Wall Street is again.

The quality of Buffett's writing, teaching, and investing are evident throughout this book. Even when he makes an effort to explain his reasoning to a less knowledgeable audience, there are instances when it only demonstrates how much ahead of his intellect is and how challenging it may be to understand some of the concepts. Nevertheless, a fantastic novel. I should add that almost all of the Berkshire Hathaway annual shareholder letters can be found at https://berkshirehathaway.com/letters/letters.html for free, but Lawrence Cunningham does a great job of distilling the information.

Security Analysis, likewise written by Benjamin Graham, is the seventh book we have. Although The Intelligent Investor has grown in popularity, in my opinion Graham's greatest work is Security Analysis. Just a lot more information is covered. And with a whopping 851 pages broken down into 52 chapters, that makes perfect sense. Everything from the psychology of investing to various assets, financial statements, portfolio structure, and stock market movements will be covered. If you decide to purchase the book, I do suggest getting the 1940 second edition. In Chapter 2, it is discussed how crucial it is to examine both qualitative and quantitative data when looking for the best results. It is examined how these two differ in terms of their worth and correctness.

The importance of current-asset values in public market corporations is discussed in Chapter 43. The essential component for screening for net-net stocks, as Benjamin Graham was known to do, is this.

And chapter 50, which discusses the differences between stock prices and values that can occasionally occur. The chapter discusses where discrepancies are most likely to be found, how to be more certain that you've found them, and an alternative strategy for stock market investing that I haven't personally looked into all that much but that does seem promising from a reversion to the mean perspective: Buy businesses that are already experiencing financial difficulties rather than those that are likely to do so in the future.
